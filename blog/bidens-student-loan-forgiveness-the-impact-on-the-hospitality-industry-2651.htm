Biden’s Student Loan Forgiveness: The Impact on the Hospitality Industry

  • Home
  • Blog
  • Blog Details

The hospitality industry has long been a cornerstone of the American economy, employing millions of workers across restaurants, hotels, and tourism sectors. Yet, it’s also an industry plagued by high turnover, low wages, and financial instability—especially for workers burdened by student debt. President Biden’s student loan forgiveness plan has sparked heated debates, but its potential impact on hospitality workers deserves closer examination. Could debt relief be the lifeline this struggling industry needs?

The Hospitality Industry’s Labor Crisis

Staffing Shortages and High Turnover

Even before the pandemic, the hospitality sector faced challenges retaining workers. Long hours, unpredictable schedules, and often minimal benefits made it difficult to attract and retain talent. The COVID-19 pandemic exacerbated these issues, with many workers leaving the industry altogether for better-paying or more stable jobs.

Now, as travel rebounds and dining demand surges, businesses are struggling to fill positions. A National Restaurant Association report found that 62% of operators say recruiting employees is their top challenge. Hotels face similar struggles, with housekeeping and front-desk roles remaining understaffed.

The Student Debt Burden on Workers

Many hospitality workers—especially those in management or culinary roles—carry student debt from degrees in hospitality management, culinary arts, or business. According to the Federal Reserve, nearly 45 million Americans owe student loans, with the average borrower carrying over $37,000 in debt.

For a line cook making $35,000 a year or a hotel manager earning $50,000, monthly loan payments can be crippling. This financial strain pushes many to leave the industry for higher-paying jobs, worsening the labor shortage.

How Student Loan Forgiveness Could Help

Immediate Financial Relief for Workers

Biden’s plan to forgive up to $20,000 in federal student loans (or $10,000 for most borrowers) could provide immediate relief. For a bartender with $25,000 in debt, wiping out a significant portion means extra cash for rent, groceries, or even career advancement courses.

This could lead to:
- Higher retention rates – Workers may stay in hospitality longer if they’re not forced to chase higher salaries elsewhere.
- Increased disposable income – More spending power could boost travel and dining demand, benefiting the industry as a whole.

Encouraging Education Without the Debt Trap

Many hospitality professionals avoid further education due to cost. Loan forgiveness, combined with reforms like income-driven repayment plans, could make culinary schools and hospitality programs more accessible.

For example, a sous chef hesitant to take on debt for a management degree might now reconsider, knowing their loans won’t be a lifelong burden. This could help fill leadership gaps in the industry.

Potential Challenges and Criticisms

Will It Really Fix the Labor Shortage?

Critics argue that loan forgiveness doesn’t address the root causes of the hospitality labor crisis—low wages and poor working conditions. Even with debt relief, a server earning $15/hour may still struggle to afford housing in major cities.

Others worry that forgiveness is a one-time fix, not a long-term solution. Without systemic changes—like higher wages or better benefits—workers may still leave for other industries.

Inflation and Economic Uncertainty

Some economists warn that widespread debt cancellation could fuel inflation, making everyday expenses (like dining out or hotel stays) more expensive. If consumer prices rise, demand for hospitality services could drop, offsetting any benefits from loan forgiveness.

The Bigger Picture: A More Stable Workforce?

If student loan forgiveness helps retain workers and attract new talent, the hospitality industry could see long-term benefits:
- Better-trained staff – More workers might pursue certifications or degrees, improving service quality.
- Stronger middle-class workforce – Debt-free employees could invest in homes or start businesses, boosting local economies.

While not a silver bullet, Biden’s plan could be a crucial step in revitalizing an industry that’s vital to both the economy and American culture. The real test will be whether it’s paired with broader reforms to make hospitality careers sustainable for the long haul.
